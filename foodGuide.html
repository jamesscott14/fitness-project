FOOD GUIDE


WHAT I NEED to eat and WHY

The Three MACROS

Macronutrients provide calories, or energy. Simply put, these are nutrients our body needs to survive.

Protein


The first MACROnutrient. It's really the HANDYMAN of the macros. When we think of protein, we think of it as the BUILDER and REPAIRMAN of our bodies. It's responsible for building and repairing things such as red blood cells, skin, hair, muscles, etc;

What does it do?

This MACRO is vital in order for our body to grow, and to aid in its maintenance. It's found in all cells of the body and the most important component in building our body's structure.

How does protein build muscle?

After a weight-lifting workout, the body needs to repair muscle cells and in order to grow, it needs to build new muscle. This is where protein comes in. Protein is made up of amino-acids which the body uses to build muscle. So when a person eats protein, their body breaks the protein down into amino-acids which in turn travel throughout the body and build new muscle cells.

Sources of Protein

    MEAT

  • Steak

    • Protein per 100 grams: 21g -28

    Different cuts equal different stats. Rib eye and T Bone steaks are at the lower end of the scale. Lean cuts like Flank and Blade steak are at the higher end of the protein scale. Excellent choice.

  • Chicken Breast

    • Protein per 100 grams: 31g

    This bodybuilding staple delivers more protein than other poultry cuts, which is why it should remain a constant presence in your shopping cart.

  • Pork Chops

    • Protein per 100 grams: 23g - 28g

    Again, the leaner the cut, the more protein there is. Pork chops are a strong source of protein!

  • Eggs

    • Protein per 100 grams: 13g

    The perfect breakfast should include eggs. They are high in protein and also provide the body with healthy fats and rich minerals.

SEAFOOD

  • Atlantic Salmon

    • Protein per 100 grams: 20g

    One of the best foods because it's not only rich in protein but healthy fat as well.

  • Halibut

    • Protein per 100 grams: 23

    Protein-dense and it tastes good. Never a bad choice.

  • Tuna

    • Protein per 100 grams: 30g

    Not exaclty a fan favourite, but numbers don't lie. If you're looking to eat as much protein as possible WITHOUT your wallet taking a hit, this is hands down the best option for anyone trying to eat healthy.

VEGETARIAN OPTIONS

  • Soy

    • Protein per 100 grams: 36g

    Most people believe that protein is hard to come by if you don't eat meat. However, that couldn't be farther from the truth. At 36g of protein, an excellent and healthy choice.

  • Tofu

    • Protein per 100 grams: 8g

    Very little protein for every 100 grams however, Tofu has a higher concentration of protein that most vegetarian foods. The recipe options with tofu are endless.

  • Pink Lentils

    • Protein per 100 grams: 25g

    Pink lentils are different than others in that it has a lot more protein in it. Found in most Asian cuisines, it's really a powerful source of protein.

Carbohydrates


Carbohyrates make up the second MACRO. It's the ENERGIZER. Your body digests it very quickly AND it comes in all shapes and forms making it the most available of the MACROS.

What do carbs do?

Carbs generally have several major functions in the body. They provide the body energy, break down fatty acids, prevent ketosis, and contain fibre.

Complex vs Simple Carbs

To understand the various types of carbs and how they affect the body, it is very important to remember that ALL CARBS ARE SUGAR.

When the a person ingests carbohydrates, the body breaks them down into GLUCOSE which is the primary component for energy. When a person eats too much, the body SAVES the excess glucose into BODY FAT.

Simple carbs are foods that have been modified and stripped of most nutrients and so digest quicker than simple carbs; they are one of the central causes of obesity. On the other hand, Complex Carbs are usually natural and whole foods that take longer to digest; they are the most nutrient-dense foods and absolutely essential to ANY diet plan. How quickly carbs are digested is a significant component of how the body reacts. When digestion is slow, the body is able to use energy efficiently when it needs it. In contrast, when digestion is fast, the body is shocked with an excessive amount of energy and as a result, uses what it needs and saves the rest.

When is it a good time to eat Simple Carbs?

Simple Carbs provide the body with an explosion of energy. If the timing of when it's ingested is either too early or too late, the body will have excess energy that it doesn't need at the moment and store it as fat to be used later. The right time to eat complex carbs is when your body is in need of quick energy an example being right after a workout.

Sources of Simple Carbs

  • White Bread
  • White Rice
  • Pastas
  • White Potatoe

When is it a good time to eat Complex Carbs?

Complex Carbs are natural, whole food options, very low in calories and break down slowly in the body, making them a great addition to any meal and at any time of the day. These foods are great to eat before a workout.

Carb Food Sources:

  • Vegetables

    • It's important to know that vegetables in particular are the most nutritious foods of all. They are closely associated with decreased chance of heart disease, cancer, and a plethora of other disease, illness, and injury.

  • Fruits

    • Fruits offer many nutritional benefits, they can be rich in essential vitamins and minerals to the body. However, one downside to fruit is that is full of sugar. Therefore, don't eat fruit too often but include it in your daily life.

  • Whole Grains

    • There's a lot of debate around the subject of "whole grains". Are they good or bad? The truth is, the term "whole grains" is something that was coined by the major food companies to define a wide range of foods. Brown bread, brown rice, "multi-grain bagels", etc. Some studies suggest that "whole grains" are actually much worse that "white grains". The reason being, when something like white bread or a white bagel is manufactured, the original grain is stripped of its nutrients, but whole grains go one step farther. Once grains have been stripped and turned white, food companies add a "special blend" of nutrients back into the white bread. What this means is that "whole grains" are more processed and worse for your health.

Fats

Fat is the most powerful MACRO of them all at 9 calories a gram. Fat is the most misunderstood of the macros. We often see foods at the grocery store labelled as having '0% fat' and this leads to the common misconception that eating fat leads to being fat. The truth is that fat is essential for the body to survive.

What does Fat do in the body?

Fat has many benefits. It's essential for normal growth and dvelopment, energy, absorbing vitamins, cushioning for the orgrans, cell membrance maintenance, and making food taste better!

Saturated or Un-Saturated?

The truth is that both types of fat are essential for the body since they have a different list of benefits. Saturated fat is usually solid at room temperature and can be found naturally in animal meat, plant oils, or dairy products such as cheese or milk. Un-Saturated fat is typically liquid and is found in foods such as nuts, seeds, fish, avocados, etc.

BAD Fats

The bad fat we find in the food kingdom comes from processed oils like canola oil, vegetable oil, and corn oil which, without much surprise is widely used in packaged junk foods as well at most fast food restaurants. Processed fats are bad for the body and provide absolutely no nutrients or benefits.
